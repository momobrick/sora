# Roof Profiles

## Purpose

This file defines the initial Sora roof profiles and how each profile affects visual language, architectural fit, and transition behavior.

## Profile Overview

Sora is initially envisioned in three roof profiles:

- vertical panel profile
- rectangular shingle profile
- low-profile barrel profile

All profiles use the same core responsive behavior: switching between reflect mode and absorb mode based on roof surface temperature.

The profiles allow Sora to feel appropriate across different home styles, climates, and regional architectural contexts.

## Shared Profile Rules

All Sora roof profiles should:

- read as believable residential roofing materials
- maintain consistent physical structure in both modes
- keep seams, sections, rhythm, and installation pattern stable
- support subtle wave-like surface-color transitions
- feel durable, architectural, and exterior-grade
- integrate into the home rather than appear as an applied visual effect

All Sora roof profiles should avoid:

- glowing seams
- digital screen effects
- mechanical flipping
- moving panels
- expanding, contracting, lifting, venting, or opening
- decorative pattern changes unrelated to the roof profile
- unrealistic surface texture

## Vertical Panel Profile

The vertical panel profile is a long vertical roofing profile inspired by the appearance and rhythm of standing seam or metal roofing.

It offers a clean, modern look and may be especially appropriate for:

- contemporary homes
- mountain homes
- rural homes
- modern farmhouse-style homes
- homes with simple gabled or shed roof forms

### Visual Character

The vertical panel profile should feel:

- clean
- structured
- modern
- architectural
- restrained
- durable

The vertical panel rhythm should remain visible but not overly stylized.

### Transition Direction

For the vertical panel profile, the transition wave moves across the roof zone.

The color shift travels horizontally from one side of the zone to the other while the long vertical rhythm of the panels remains visually stable.

Preferred language:

- "the transition moves across the roof zone"
- "a horizontal wave of surface-color change travels across the vertical panels"
- "the panel rhythm remains stable while the surface shifts between modes"

Avoid language:

- "panels flip over"
- "seams open"
- "metal strips move"
- "the roof rotates"

## Rectangular Shingle Profile

The rectangular shingle profile is a square or rectangular roofing profile inspired by traditional residential shingles.

It provides a familiar residential appearance and may be especially appropriate for:

- suburban homes
- cottages
- ranch homes
- traditional residential roof forms
- everyday family homes

### Visual Character

The rectangular shingle profile should feel:

- familiar
- residential
- practical
- approachable
- refined but not precious

The shingle pattern should remain realistic and consistent across both modes.

### Transition Direction

For the rectangular shingle profile, the transition wave moves down the roof zone.

The color shift travels from the upper edge toward the lower edge, following the natural downward layering of traditional shingles.

Preferred language:

- "the transition moves down the roof zone"
- "a downward wave of surface-color change follows the shingle rows"
- "the shingle structure remains stable while the surface shifts between modes"

Avoid language:

- "shingles flip"
- "rows peel back"
- "tiles slide"
- "the pattern changes during the transition"

## Low-Profile Barrel Profile

The low-profile barrel profile is a curved or subtly arched roofing profile inspired by clay, terracotta, and barrel tile roofs often used in hot, sunny climates.

It provides a regionally familiar option for:

- Southwestern homes
- Spanish Colonial homes
- Mediterranean homes
- desert homes
- sunbelt homes
- hot, sunny climate regions

### Visual Character

The low-profile barrel profile should feel:

- regionally familiar
- sun-adapted
- sculptural but restrained
- low-profile
- durable
- integrated with the home

The barrel form should be subtle and architectural, not exaggerated or decorative.

### Transition Direction

For the low-profile barrel profile, the transition wave moves down the roof zone.

The color shift travels from the upper edge toward the lower edge, following the roof slope and drainage direction of barrel-style roofing.

Preferred language:

- "the transition moves down the roof zone"
- "a downward wave of surface-color change follows the roof slope"
- "the barrel profile remains physically stable while the surface shifts between modes"

Avoid language:

- "tiles roll over"
- "barrels rotate"
- "the roof opens"
- "the tile shape changes"

## Prompting Guidance

When generating product imagery, name the roof profile clearly and describe only the surface-color response.

Example:

> Show a residential home with Sora's vertical panel roof profile. The roof is in reflect mode, with a lighter surface tone that feels integrated into the roof material. Preserve the panel rhythm, seams, roof shape, and physical structure.

For transition imagery, include the direction appropriate to the profile.

Example:

> Show a subtle horizontal wave of surface-color transition moving across the vertical panel roof zone. The panels do not move, flip, lift, open, or change shape.
