# Sora Anti-Patterns

Use this file when reviewing Sora copy, visuals, prompts, campaigns, or strategic concepts for off-brand patterns. This file defines what Sora should not become.

## Summary

Sora should not feel like luxury sustainability, a cold technology product, a fear-based climate campaign, or a literal bird-themed brand.

The brand should remain practical, warm, grounded, climate-aware, and broadly attainable.

## Verbal Anti-Patterns

### Luxury sustainability clichés

Avoid language that makes Sora feel expensive, exclusive, elite, or designed only for high-end homes.

Do not frame Sora as a premium eco-upgrade or aspirational luxury feature. The brand should feel useful across income levels and housing contexts.

### Fear-based climate messaging

Avoid leading with panic, guilt, doom, or blame.

Sora can acknowledge changing climate conditions, but the message should focus on practical adaptation, resilience, comfort, and agency.

### Political or ideologically loaded language

Avoid framing Sora through polarizing environmental language. The brand should feel relevant to a wide audience, including people who may be skeptical of climate-tech or green-energy messaging.

### Cold smart-home language

Avoid making Sora sound like a connected gadget, app platform, or complicated automation system.

Sora should feel like a building material that helps a home respond naturally, not another technology the homeowner has to manage.

### Empty launch hype

Avoid phrases like “coming soon,” “get ready,” “stay tuned,” “the future is here,” and similar generic anticipation language.

Sora’s launch language should create interest through purpose and relevance, not hype.

### Unsupported claims

Avoid claims that promise exact energy savings, eliminate heating or cooling needs, guarantee affordability, or imply outcomes that have not been established.

Use language such as “supports,” “helps,” “designed to,” and “may” when appropriate.

## Visual Anti-Patterns

### Overly futuristic aesthetics

Avoid sci-fi interfaces, glowing lines, neon effects, holograms, chrome futurism, and digital-tech styling that makes the brand feel detached from real homes.

### Generic climate-tech branding

Avoid visual clichés such as glowing globes, stock leaves, abstract green gradients, generic energy icons, and overly familiar sustainability symbolism.

### Premium-only residential imagery

Avoid imagery that makes Sora feel available only to luxury homeowners. The visual world should include believable homes and communities, not only pristine architectural showpieces.

### Literal bird imagery

Avoid feathers, wings, nests, beaks, mascot characters, or decorative bird motifs. The Sora name should inform the identity through subtle ideas of migration, orientation, seasonal change, and environmental responsiveness.

### Product spectacle

Avoid dramatizing the product as mechanical, glowing, flipping, opening, unfolding, or transforming in a sci-fi way. Sora should feel integrated, architectural, and believable.

## Strategic Anti-Patterns

### Too tech-forward

Do not position Sora as technology for technology’s sake. The brand’s innovation should always connect back to comfort, resilience, efficiency, and everyday usefulness.

### Too abstract

Do not make Sora feel like a vague climate philosophy. The brand should remain connected to homes, materials, places, and practical adaptation.

### Too niche

Do not make Sora feel useful only in one climate, region, income level, or architectural style. The brand should feel adaptable across real-world conditions.

### Too experimental

Do not make Sora feel fragile, conceptual, or speculative in a way that undermines trust. Even when presented as a speculative project, the brand language should make the idea feel reliable and grounded.

## Product Representation Anti-Patterns

At the brand level, avoid detailed product mechanics unless needed. When product behavior is mentioned, do not misrepresent Sora as:

- A paint, coating, film, or add-on finish
- A solar energy generation product
- A roof that physically flips, opens, lifts, vents, rotates, unfolds, expands, or contracts
- A complete replacement for heating, cooling, insulation, or ventilation
- A luxury smart-home gadget
- A product that responds to general weather rather than the conditions experienced by the material

Detailed product rules should live in the product folder, but all brand-facing work should avoid these misrepresentations.

## Review Question

Before approving Sora work, ask:

Does this make climate-adaptive building materials feel practical, human, reliable, and within reach?

If the answer is no, the work may be drifting into an anti-pattern.
