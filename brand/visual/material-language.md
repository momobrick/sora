# Material Language

Sora’s primary material signature is a soft sculptural wave texture with subtle striated surface detail.

This material language should appear across all Sora roof profiles, adapting to the geometry of each profile while remaining recognizably part of the same system.

## Core Signature

The Sora surface should feel:

- Smooth
- Sculptural
- Integrated
- Architectural
- Low-relief
- Gently responsive
- Durable and practical
- Material, not graphic

The surface should suggest environmental movement, temperature response, roof zones, airflow, migration paths, and gradual transition without becoming illustrative or decorative.

## Wave Structure

The wave pattern should use broad, flowing bands with soft organic movement.

Use:

- Wide flowing bands
- Shallow relief
- Flat softened crests
- Wide rounded valleys
- Soft transitions between high and low areas
- Calm, elongated movement
- Low contrast shadows and highlights

Avoid:

- Sharp peaks
- Deep grooves
- Tight waves
- Jittery curves
- Decorative swirls
- Dramatic ridges
- Overly repetitive patterning
- Anything that feels molded plastic or artificial

## Striated Surface Detail

Fine striations are part of the Sora material signature.

They should follow the direction of the wave flow and add subtle surface depth without becoming noisy or rough.

Use:

- Fine linear texture
- Soft directional grain
- Low-contrast detail
- Integrated surface movement
- Gentle material variation

Avoid:

- Grit
- Speckling
- Roughness
- High-contrast lines
- Printed graphics
- Obvious pattern overlays
- Metallic shine
- Glossy plastic texture

## Finish

Sora roof material should have a smooth satin-matte or low-sheen finish.

The finish should feel refined and durable, but not glossy, metallic, plastic, or decorative.

The surface may catch light softly along crests and valleys, but highlights should remain gentle and believable.

## Application Across Roof Profiles

The wave and striation system should adapt to each roof profile.

### Vertical Panel Profile

On vertical panels, the wave texture should flow with the panel direction. Pattern movement should remain contained within each panel pan. Standing seams or panel ribs should act as boundaries.

Do not stretch one continuous wave pattern across the full roof if it ignores the panel structure.

### Rectangular Shingle Profile

On rectangular shingles, the wave texture should adapt to the shingle unit. The pattern may repeat across shingles, but it should remain subtle and integrated.

The shingle grid should stay readable. The material texture should not erase the roof profile.

### Low-Profile Barrel Profile

On low-profile barrel forms, the wave texture should follow the curved roof geometry. The sculptural surface should feel molded into the profile rather than applied on top.

Avoid exaggerated ridges or overly decorative curvature.

## Reflect Mode

In Reflect Mode, the material should appear light, warm, and mineral.

Use:

- Warm light gray
- Soft mineral gray
- Pale stone
- Light taupe-gray undertones
- Subtle shadows
- Quiet surface definition

Reflect Mode should feel bright and heat-reducing without becoming stark white, icy silver, or synthetic.

## Absorb Mode

In Absorb Mode, the material should appear deep, warm, and grounded.

Use:

- Warm charcoal
- Dark graphite
- Smoked bronze undertones
- Subtle caramel or bronze warmth
- Gentle highlights
- Soft shadow depth

Absorb Mode should feel heat-retaining without becoming black plastic, glossy metal, or heavy industrial roofing.

## Transition Behavior

When Sora shifts between Reflect Mode and Absorb Mode, the change should appear as a subtle wave-like transition across the roof surface.

The transition should feel integrated into the material system, not like a digital animation, graphic overlay, or mechanical transformation.

The material itself does not physically reshape. The visible change is a surface color and mode shift.

## Brand Use Beyond Roofing

The Sora material language may inspire subtle graphic devices across the broader brand system.

It can inform:

- Curved dividers
- Soft background bands
- Image masks
- Transition zones
- Restrained linework
- Surface-inspired layouts

These uses should remain sophisticated and quiet. The material language should support the brand, not become decoration.

## Avoid

Do not use material treatments that feel:

- Futuristic
- Sci-fi
- Neon
- Metallic
- Glossy
- Plastic
- Overly decorative
- Mechanically transformed
- Generic roofing texture
- Printed pattern
- Digital interface effect

Sora’s material language should always feel like a real architectural roofing material that has been designed to respond to changing environmental conditions.
