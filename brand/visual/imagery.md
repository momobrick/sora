# Imagery

Sora imagery should feel photorealistic, residential, climate-specific, and system-built. The visual style should make responsive roofing feel believable and practical, not speculative, futuristic, or decorative.

Sora imagery is not generic architectural photography. It is a controlled visual system built from believable homes, specific climate environments, clear roof visibility, material-derived color behavior, and restrained transition effects.

## Core Direction

Sora imagery should feel:

- Photorealistic
- Residential
- Climate-aware
- Warm and grounded
- Practical rather than spectacular
- Slightly refined, but not luxury-oriented
- Art-directed, but not artificial
- Believable before it feels branded

The best Sora imagery should look like a real home in a real environment, with a subtle responsive material treatment applied to the roof and lighting.

## Image Types

### Architectural Study Imagery

Architectural study images are used to develop, compare, or explain house forms and roof applications.

Use these when the goal is to isolate the home, roof, profile, or product behavior without environmental distraction.

Study imagery should use:

- A minimal neutral architectural staging environment
- A flat matte neutral ground plane
- A clean horizon line
- A slightly lighter neutral background above the horizon
- Soft, even lighting
- Subtle contact shadows
- No landscape, roads, people, decor, signage, or environmental detail

The purpose is clarity. These images should feel like clean architectural studies, not final campaign visuals.

### Environmental Campaign Imagery

Environmental campaign images show Sora in context. These images place believable homes into climate-specific residential environments.

Use these when the goal is to communicate climate adaptation, regional relevance, or the Reflect Mode / Absorb Mode story.

Environmental imagery should include:

- A real-feeling home
- A specific climate environment
- A visible roof plane
- Natural scale and perspective
- Believable contact shadows
- Clear environmental detail
- A refined responsive lighting and roof-color treatment when appropriate

The environment should support the product story without overpowering the home.

## Residential Character

Homes should feel ordinary enough to be believable and refined enough to support the Sora brand.

Use homes that are:

- Well-built
- Regionally appropriate
- Residential in scale
- Slightly refined
- Practical and grounded
- Climate-appropriate
- Architecturally clear

Avoid homes that feel:

- Overly luxurious
- Resort-like
- Themed
- Futuristic
- Decorative
- Overly rustic
- Too polished or render-like
- Like real estate listing photography
- Like fantasy architecture

Sora should feel like a material system that could be adopted broadly, not a product only for high-end custom homes.

## Climate Context

Sora imagery should show the product working across different climates and regions.

Approved climate environments include:

- Southwestern desert
- Rocky Mountain forest
- Tropical coast
- Midwest farmland
- Pacific Northwest forest

Climate context should be specific and recognizable, but not theatrical. Use natural environmental cues such as vegetation, terrain, sky, horizon, and regional materials.

Avoid generic “green” sustainability imagery. Do not rely on symbolic nature clichés, glowing leaves, tech overlays, or abstract environmental graphics.

## Roof Visibility

The roof is the primary storytelling surface for Sora.

Imagery should prioritize compositions where the roof plane is clearly visible and large enough to show product behavior.

Roof surfaces should generally be:

- Broad
- Simple
- Clean
- Clearly defined
- Uninterrupted
- Easy to read
- Believable for the home style and region

Avoid roof surfaces that are visually cluttered or difficult to edit.

Do not add unnecessary:

- Solar panels
- Skylights
- Vents
- Decorative trim
- Strong roof texture
- Busy roof patterns
- Dominant chimneys
- Ornamental roof features

A modest chimney may appear only when it is architecturally appropriate and does not distract from the roof system.

## Composition

Preferred Sora imagery uses an elevated three-quarter architectural view.

The camera should be slightly above eye level and angled gently downward so the roof plane is visible without feeling exaggerated.

The home or site should recede diagonally through the composition, creating depth and architectural perspective.

Compositions should show:

- The primary roof plane
- The front facade
- The entry or porch when relevant
- Enough side elevation to show depth
- Enough environment to establish climate context

Avoid straight-on elevations, extreme aerial views, dramatic cinematic angles, or compositions where the roof is hidden.

## Lighting

Base imagery should begin with bright neutral overcast daylight when possible. This provides soft, even lighting, crisp detail, and minimal directional shadow, making the image easier to adapt.

Final campaign imagery may use more expressive lighting, especially the Sora divided-light treatment.

### Reflect Mode Lighting

Reflect Mode is associated with bright daylight and heat reduction.

Use:

- Clear daylight
- Warm light-gray roof color
- Natural daylight shadows
- Crisp, realistic environmental detail
- A calm but bright atmosphere

### Absorb Mode Lighting

Absorb Mode is associated with cooler conditions, dusk, dawn, or low sun exposure.

Use:

- Dusk or dawn atmosphere
- Warm charcoal roof color
- Softened or reduced shadows
- Subtle warm interior window glow when visible
- A calm, diffuse evening feeling

Avoid overly theatrical lighting, hard color filters, neon glows, or dramatic fantasy skies.

## Divided-Light Treatment

The divided-light treatment is a primary Sora product-storytelling style.

It shows Reflect Mode and Absorb Mode in one image using a controlled transition across the roof, house, sky, and environment.

Use this treatment when the goal is to communicate the product’s responsive behavior quickly.

The divided-light treatment should include:

- Reflect Mode on the daylight side
- Absorb Mode on the dusk side
- A broad organic curvilinear transition boundary
- A unified sky structure
- Believable lighting changes
- Preserved architecture and environment
- Subtle warm window light on the dusk side when appropriate

The transition should feel environmental and material, not graphic.

Do not add:

- A visible line or stroke
- A white, black, or colored border
- A glow, halo, rim, or seam
- Two mismatched skies
- New roof patterns
- New architectural features
- New objects, people, icons, or text

The image should still feel like the same house in the same environment.

## Transition Boundary

Sora’s transition boundary should be broad, fluid, organic, and gently sculptural.

It may move across the composition with a subtle diagonal drift. It should span the image cleanly and feel connected to material response, roof zones, environmental thresholds, and the movement of climate conditions.

The boundary should be defined by changes in lighting and roof color, not by an added graphic mark.

Avoid curves that feel:

- Jittery
- Tight
- Decorative
- Cartoonish
- Like a spotlight
- Like a transparent overlay
- Like a Photoshop mask
- Like a hard graphic divider

## Sky and Environment

Sora skies should feel photographic and continuous.

When using the divided-light treatment, the cloud structure should remain unified across the image. Only lighting and color temperature should change between sides.

Avoid:

- Mismatched cloud formations
- Two separate sky systems
- Overly dramatic fantasy skies
- Plastic-looking landscapes
- Repeated AI textures
- Artificial vegetation
- Overprocessed environmental detail

The environment should remain real, grounded, and climate-specific.

## Image Quality Standards

Sora imagery should preserve realism above all else.

When editing or generating imagery:

- Preserve the base house
- Preserve the landscape
- Preserve the camera angle
- Preserve the roof form
- Preserve the original roof material or pattern unless intentionally replacing it with a Sora roof profile
- Preserve windows, doors, porch structure, and architectural details
- Preserve natural scale and contact shadows

Only change what is necessary to communicate Sora’s product behavior.

## Avoid

Avoid imagery that feels:

- Sci-fi
- Futuristic
- Digitally overlaid
- Neon
- Synthetic
- Hyper-luxury
- Generic “green tech”
- Real estate staged
- Overly scenic
- Decorative
- Overly symbolic
- AI-generated in an obvious way

Do not use:

- Literal bird imagery
- Migration graphics as decoration
- Glowing roof effects
- Data-interface overlays
- Floating icons
- People pointing at roofs
- Product spectacle
- Unrealistic climate drama

## Summary

Sora imagery should make climate-responsive roofing feel practical, beautiful, and believable.

The visual system is built from real-feeling homes, specific climate environments, visible roof planes, warm material-derived color, and restrained transition behavior.

The result should feel like a refined residential campaign for a product that belongs in the built environment.
