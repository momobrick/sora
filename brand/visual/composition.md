# Composition

Sora composition should feel calm, clear, architectural, and purposeful.

Composition should help people understand the product: a responsive roofing material that shifts between Reflect Mode and Absorb Mode in response to roof surface temperature.

The roof is often the primary storytelling surface, so compositions should support roof visibility without becoming dramatic or artificial.

## Core Principles

Sora compositions should feel:

- Clear
- Calm
- Balanced
- Grounded
- Architectural
- Purposeful
- Residential
- Climate-aware
- Believable

Composition should clarify adaptation, not dramatize it.

Avoid compositions that feel:

- Cinematic for no reason
- Overly spectacular
- Sci-fi
- Luxury real estate
- Too abstract
- Too diagrammatic
- Visually cluttered
- Disconnected from homes or climate context

## Architectural Composition

When showing homes, use an elevated three-quarter architectural view.

The camera should be slightly above eye level and angled gently downward so the roof plane is clearly visible.

Preferred architectural composition:

- Elevated front-left or front-right three-quarter view
- Roof plane clearly visible
- Front facade visible
- Entry or porch visible when relevant
- Side elevation visible enough to show depth
- Home receding diagonally through the composition
- Environment present but not overpowering
- Natural scale and perspective

The home or site may recede from lower left to upper right to create depth and a consistent image series.

Avoid:

- Straight-on elevations
- Extreme aerial views
- Low heroic angles
- Overly dramatic perspective
- Crops that hide the roof
- Real estate listing compositions
- Empty product renders without context when storytelling is needed

## Roof-First Framing

The roof should be composed as a meaningful surface.

Use roof-first framing when the goal is to show:

- Reflect Mode
- Absorb Mode
- Roof zones
- Transition behavior
- Roof profile
- Material texture
- Climate response

The roof should be large enough to read, but the home should still feel whole and residential.

Avoid making the roof look detached from the house or treated as a floating product surface.

## Environmental Composition

Sora imagery should include enough environment to establish climate context.

The environment should support the story without becoming the subject.

Use:

- Regional vegetation
- Climate-specific terrain
- Believable horizon
- Natural sky
- Clear buildable ground plane
- Crisp environmental detail
- Calm atmosphere

Avoid:

- Scenic landscape spectacle
- Dramatic disaster imagery
- Overcrowded vegetation
- Roads, vehicles, signs, fences, or people unless intentionally needed
- Backgrounds that feel generated, plastic, or repetitive

## Divided-Light Composition

The divided-light treatment is a key Sora composition style.

It should show Reflect Mode and Absorb Mode within one coherent image.

The composition should include:

- Daylight / Reflect Mode on one side
- Dusk / Absorb Mode on the other side
- One broad organic transition boundary
- A continuous sky and environment
- Preserved architecture
- A roof plane that clearly shows both modes

The transition boundary should move through the composition with calm, organic flow.

It may have a slight diagonal drift.

The boundary should not look like:

- A graphic stroke
- A hard decorative line
- A glowing seam
- A spotlight
- A circular overlay
- A Photoshop mask
- A separate layer pasted on top

The shift should be defined by lighting and material color, not by an added outline.

## Graphic Layout Composition

Sora’s material and transition language may also guide layouts.

Use subtle curves, bands, masks, and transition zones to create calm movement across:

- Slides
- Campaign panels
- Website sections
- Product diagrams
- Social layouts
- Captions
- Callout systems

Graphic composition should feel sophisticated and restrained.

Use transition forms to support hierarchy, not decorate empty space.

## Layout Behavior

Sora layouts should use:

- Generous margins
- Calm alignment
- Clear hierarchy
- Warm negative space
- Subtle asymmetry
- Broad curves
- Soft transitions
- Material-inspired rhythm
- Clear relationship between text and image

Avoid:

- Busy layouts
- Loud graphic overlays
- Excessive pattern
- High-contrast diagonal slashes
- Generic tech grids
- Floating UI panels
- Decorative climate icons
- Overly complex compositions

## Diagonal Movement

Diagonal movement can help Sora compositions feel adaptive and architectural.

Use diagonal recession through:

- Roof planes
- Site perspective
- Sun paths
- Shadow paths
- Transition boundaries
- Environmental layering

Diagonal movement should feel natural, not aggressive.

Avoid making the brand feel fast, sharp, or unstable.

## Curves and Transition Zones

Curves should be broad, fluid, and gently sculptural.

Use curves to suggest:

- Environmental thresholds
- Material response
- Roof-zone transitions
- Temperature shifts
- Migration-informed movement
- Seasonal adaptation

Curves should be restrained and purposeful.

Avoid:

- Decorative waves
- Tight wavy lines
- Jittery motion
- Cartoon curves
- Generic organic blobs
- Excessive contour patterns
- Literal migration maps

## Information Composition

When explaining product behavior, compositions should remain clear and simple.

Use diagrams and callouts to clarify:

- Reflect Mode
- Absorb Mode
- Roof zones
- Surface temperature trigger
- Transition behavior
- Manual override
- Sync all behavior

Product diagrams should feel architectural and material-based, not like software dashboards.

Avoid making Sora feel like a complex smart-home system.

## Summary

Sora composition should make adaptation easy to see and easy to believe.

The strongest compositions show real homes, clear roof planes, climate context, and subtle material transitions with calm architectural control.
