# House Landscape Composite

## Purpose

Place a Sora regional house study into a compatible landscape background plate while preserving the house design, landscape realism, roof visibility, and compositing-friendly roof surface.

This skill connects two earlier Sora image workflow steps:

- `regional-house-study.md` creates the house.
- `landscape-background-plate.md` creates the empty environment.

Use this skill to combine them into one believable photorealistic residential scene.

## Use this skill when

- You have a house image created with the Regional House Study skill.
- You have a landscape image created with the Landscape Background Plate skill.
- You want the house placed naturally into the landscape.
- You need a realistic house/environment image before applying the Divided Lighting skill.
- You want the house and landscape to feel like they were photographed together.

## Do not use this skill when

- You need to generate a house from scratch.
- You need to generate an empty landscape plate from scratch.
- You want to apply Reflect Mode and Absorb Mode lighting.
- You want to redesign the house or landscape.
- You want to match one finished image series to another by changing camera angle only.

## Core task

Use the house image and the landscape image together. Place the house into the clear buildable area of the landscape while preserving the identity of both source images.

The final image should look like one realistic residential scene, not a pasted object or a newly redesigned house.

## Required outcome

The final composite should show:

- one preserved regional house
- one preserved regional landscape
- a believable level building site
- consistent perspective
- natural scale
- realistic contact shadows
- clear roof visibility
- a clean roof surface ready for later Sora treatment

## Inputs

- A regional house study image
- A landscape background plate image
- Optional regional pairing guidance
- Optional composition direction
- Optional roof visibility requirements

## Compatible pairings

This skill works best when house and landscape belong to the same or a believable related region:

- Southwestern desert house + Southwestern desert landscape
- Rocky Mountain cabin + Rocky Mountain forest landscape
- Tropical coastal house + Tropical coast landscape
- Midwest farmhouse or rural home + Midwest farmland landscape
- Pacific Northwest forest home + Pacific Northwest forest landscape

Cross-pairing is allowed only when the result remains architecturally and environmentally believable.

## Instructions

1. Use the house image as the architectural source.
2. Use the landscape image as the environmental source.
3. Place the house into the level buildable area of the landscape.
4. Preserve the house design as much as possible.
5. Preserve the landscape as much as possible.
6. Match the house to the landscape perspective.
7. Match the house scale to the site.
8. Align the base of the house with the ground plane.
9. Add realistic contact shadows beneath and around the house.
10. Integrate the foundation, porch, stairs, or raised structure naturally with the terrain.
11. Keep the main roof plane visible and readable.
12. Keep the roof simple, clean, and unobtrusive.
13. Maintain soft, neutral overcast lighting unless otherwise requested.
14. Keep the scene photorealistic and physically believable.

## House preservation rules

Do not redesign or substantially change:

- the house style
- massing
- roof form
- porch layout
- railings
- stairs
- windows
- doors
- foundation type
- exterior materials
- regional architectural character

Minor perspective adjustment is allowed only when necessary to make the house sit believably in the landscape.

## Landscape preservation rules

Do not regenerate or substantially change:

- the landscape type
- existing trees or planting character
- distant background
- horizon structure
- ground plane direction
- lighting mood
- camera angle
- overall composition

Minor localized ground adjustment is allowed only around the house placement area so the house feels physically integrated.

## Roof rules

The roof should remain:

- visible
- clean
- simple
- broad enough for later Sora treatment
- free from new visual clutter

Do not add:

- solar panels
- skylights
- vents
- ornate roof features
- strong roofing texture
- new roof pattern
- unnecessary chimneys

A modest chimney may be included only if it already exists in the house source or is specifically requested and architecturally appropriate.

## Site integration rules

The house should sit on a believable buildable area.

The area beneath and immediately around the house should be flat or only gently graded. Avoid placing the house on a dramatic slope, unstable ground plane, or terrain that makes the structure feel unsupported.

Use natural details sparingly to integrate the house, such as:

- subtle grading
- contact shadows
- small ground transitions
- native grasses or planting near the foundation
- exposed soil or gravel near the base
- believable porch, stair, or foundation contact with the ground

Do not let foreground plants, rocks, or landscape elements block the house or obscure the roof.

## Lighting and realism rules

Use consistent lighting across the house and landscape:

- same general light direction
- same softness or shadow quality
- same atmospheric clarity
- same color temperature
- believable contact shadows
- realistic scale and perspective

The composite should not look like a cutout, collage, rendering overlay, or AI-rebuilt scene.

## Guardrails

- Do not create a new house design.
- Do not replace the landscape.
- Do not change the regional climate setting.
- Do not over-landscape the site.
- Do not add people, vehicles, roads, fences, signs, text, outdoor decor, construction equipment, or other buildings.
- Do not make the home feel like a resort, luxury estate, or fantasy architecture.
- Do not obscure the roof.
- Do not add Sora Reflect Mode or Absorb Mode treatment in this step.

## Success criteria

A successful output:

- feels like a real house photographed in a real place
- preserves the source house design
- preserves the source landscape character
- places the house on a stable buildable area
- uses believable scale, perspective, and contact shadows
- keeps the roof visible for later Sora product visualization
- feels consistent with the broader Sora image system

## Prompt scaffold

Use the house image as the architectural source and the landscape image as the environmental source. Place the house into the clear level buildable area of the landscape while preserving both images as much as possible.

Preserve the house design, massing, roof form, porch layout, windows, doors, foundation type, exterior materials, and regional architectural character. Do not redesign the house or turn it into a different style.

Preserve the landscape type, planting character, distant background, lighting mood, camera angle, and overall realism. Do not regenerate or replace the landscape.

Match the house to the landscape perspective, scale, ground plane, and lighting. Align the house naturally with the level buildable area and add believable contact shadows so it feels physically grounded. Make any terrain adjustment subtle and localized to the area directly beneath and around the house.

Keep the main roof plane clearly visible and clean for later Sora roof treatment. Do not add solar panels, skylights, vents, strong roof texture, new roof pattern, ornate roof details, or unnecessary chimneys.

The final image should look like one photorealistic residential scene: the same regional house placed naturally into the same regional landscape, with consistent perspective, natural scale, soft overcast lighting, and believable environmental integration.
